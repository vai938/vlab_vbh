Aim

To perform quantitative tests for carbohydrate.

Apparatus

Principle

Carbohydrates are important constituent of all living cells. Carbohydrates, one of the four major classes of biomolecules along with proteins, nucleic acids, and lipids. Carbohydrates are aldehyde or ketone compounds with multiple hydroxyl groups. sThese are defined as polyhydroxy aldehydes or ketones. Carbohydrates are polyhydroxy aldehydes or ketones, or substances that yield such compounds on hydrolysis. Many, but not all, carbohydrates have the empirical formula (CH2O)n .

    Classification of Carbohydrate:

  1. Monosaccharides

    Monosaccharides are those carbohydrates that cannot be hydrolyzed into simpler carbohydrates: They may be classified as trioses, tetroses, pentoses, hexoses, or heptoses, depending upon the number of carbon atoms; and as aldoses or ketoses depending upon whether they have an aldehyde or ketone group.

  2. Disaccharides

    Disaccharides are condensation products of two monosaccharide units. Examples are maltose and sucrose.

  3. Oligosaccharides

    Oligosaccharides are condensation products of two to ten monosaccharides; maltotriose* is an example

  4. Polysaccharides

    Polysaccharides are condensation products of more than ten monosaccharide units; examples are the starches and dextrins, which may be linear or branched polymers. Polysaccharides are sometimes classified as hexosans or pentosans, depending upon the identity of the constituent monosaccharides.

Procedure

  1. MOLISCH TEST

    3 ml of carbohydrate solution was taken in a test-tube and added 2 drops of Molisch reagent and mixed. Inclined the tube and to this added 2 ml of conc. H2SO4, along the sides of the test-tube.

  2. BENEDICT’S TEST

    2 ml of carbohydrate solution was taken and added 2 ml of the Benedict’s reagent. Mixed the contents and boiled the mixture in the water bath for 2-3 minutes.

  3. BARFOED’S TEST

    2 ml of Barfoed’s reagent was taken and added 2 ml of Carbohydrate solution. Mixed the contents and boiled in the water bath initially for 2-3 minutes. If a brick red precipitate which settle at bottom is formed, it indicates the presence of reducing monosaccharide however, if no precipitate is formed within 2-3 minutes then boiling is continued for another 10 minutes. If there is formation of a brick red precipitate which settles at the bottom of the tube indicating the presence of a reducing disaccharide.

  4. SELIWANOFF’S TEST

    2ml of Selvanoff’s reagent was taken and added 8-10 drops of carbohydrate solution. Mixed the contents and boiled the mixture first for 45 sec to 1 minute. If the red colour is formed within this time then it is Fructose.

Observations & Result

  1. MOLISCH TEST

    Violet ring is formed at the junction.

  2. BENEDICT’S TEST

    A reddish yellow precipitate is observed given by reducing sugars.

  3. BARFOED’S TEST

    In reducing monosaccharide precipitate is formed within 2-3minutes while in reducing disaccharide brick red precipitate is formed in 10-15 minutes.

  4. SELIWANOFF’S TEST

    Red colour is obtained in fructose only.

Discussion

  • MOLISCH TEST

    This reaction is due to the formation of furfural and its derivatives such as hydroxyl methyl furfural which is formed by the action of the acid acting on the sugar since this test is given by all furfural yielding substances therefore it is general test for all carbohydrates.

  • BENEDICT’S TEST

    This test is helpful in detecting a reducing sugar which has a free ketonic or free aldehyde group. Therefore, when a reducing sugar is heated with Benedicts reagent then the free keto or aldehyde groups reduce Cupric to Cuprous oxide in the form of a reddish yellow precipitate. However, sucrose and starch give negative results because of absence of free ketone or aldehyde groups.

  • BARFOED’S TEST

    This test is important in differentiating a reducing monosaccharide from a reducing disaccharide. This is also a copper-reduction test difference is that Barfoeds test is performed using weak acidic solution. For reducing monosaccharides the precipitate is formed within 2-3 minutes while for the reducing disaccharide it is formed within 10-15 minutes of boiling. Also the amount of the precipitate for disaccharide is much less than in monosaccharides.

  • SELIWANOFF’S TEST

    This test is specific for Fructose because of the presence of free ketone group. The reaction is due to the conversion of fructose (levulose) into levulinic acid + Hydroxy methyl furfural. Later the latter condenses with Resorcinol present in the selivanoff’s reagent to give red colour. This test is also given by sucrose after prolonged heating for 2-3minutes. Since during this boiling sucrose gets hydrolysed to give fructose as one of the products.
